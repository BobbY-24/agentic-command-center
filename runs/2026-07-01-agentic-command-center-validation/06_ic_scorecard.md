# IC Scorecard

Scoring scale:

```text
1 = weak / unfavorable
3 = moderate
5 = strong / favorable
```

Clarifications:

- Reversibility: higher score means easier to test or undo.
- Execution cost: higher score means cheaper/easier, not more expensive.
- Risk: higher score means lower risk.
- Human-data dependency: higher score means less blocked by missing human data.

| Option / Team | Upside | Evidence strength | Reversibility | Execution cost | Career/research value | Risk | Human-data dependency | Overall judgment |
| ------------- | -----: | ----------------: | ------------: | -------------: | --------------------: | ---: | --------------------: | ---------------- |
| Bull / Builder - continue developing | 5 | 3 | 3 | 3 | 5 | 3 | 3 | Promising, but not yet proven by enough real usage. |
| Bear / Skeptic - simplify or park | 3 | 4 | 5 | 5 | 2 | 5 | 4 | Sensible guardrail, but too conservative before a real project test. |
| Operator / MVP - small test | 4 | 4 | 5 | 5 | 4 | 5 | 4 | Best risk-adjusted path: test usefulness without more meta-work. |

## Highest-leverage uncertainty

Will Bob voluntarily use the command center on a real research or career idea because it improves the decision and execution, not because the system itself is interesting?

## Evidence that would change the decision

- A real run produces a clearer decision question than Bob started with.
- A real run creates a Codex task spec that leads to better implementation.
- Bob discards at least one artifact as unnecessary, proving the workflow can simplify itself.
- Bob would use the system again without needing to justify it.

Evidence that would reverse the recommendation:

- The next real run takes too long and produces no better decision.
- The memos feel ceremonial.
- The system encourages more prompt-writing instead of execution.

## Recommended IC outcome

small test
